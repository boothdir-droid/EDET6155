"Show, Don't Tell"

an original scene — in the style of Aaron Sorkin

characters: MARCUS COLE, DIANE OKAFOR, PRIYA NAIR, JOEL FITCH, DR. SAMUEL PARK

INT. BREAKERS HOTEL — HARBOR BALLROOM C — AFTERNOON
A breakout session at the National Education Technology Summit. Maybe two hundred chairs, maybe ninety of them filled. A BANNER reads: "Learning in the Age of AI: Promise, Peril, and Possibility." A podium. A screen. A clicker.
MARCUS COLE, 41, co-founder of a company called Luminary, strides to the podium in the way that says he's rehearsed not striding to the podium. He doesn't use the podium. He steps in front of it.
MARCUS
I'm going to start with a question, and I want you to hold your answer — don't say it out loud, just hold it. When was the last time a student surprised you? Not got a good score. Not followed directions well. Surprised you. A student did something you didn't expect and you thought — oh. There you are.
Beat. He looks at the room.
MARCUS (CONT'D)
Hold that. We'll come back to it.
He clicks. The screen changes: a graph of student engagement metrics, 2010 to present.
MARCUS (CONT'D)
This is what forty billion dollars buys you. Forty billion dollars invested in educational technology over the last decade, and we got — this. A line that goes sideways. We got Chromebook carts in hallways. We got licensing fees and help desk tickets and teachers who figured out how to use a new LMS six months before someone decided to switch to a different one.
DIANE OKAFOR, 55, a veteran high school principal from outside Columbus, shifts in her seat. She's heard this before.
DIANE
That's an interesting slide to open with, given that you sell ed tech.
A few laughs. Marcus doesn't miss a beat.
MARCUS
Yes it is. Thank you for saying it out loud, because I was getting to it and you saved me a minute and a half. I sell ed tech and I'm telling you most ed tech has failed, because the people building it — and I am including myself in this, I am standing inside this critique — spent ten years asking the wrong question.
DIANE
And the wrong question was—
MARCUS
"How do we get students to engage with the technology?" When the question should have been "how does the technology get out of the way so students can engage with each other and with ideas?"
JOEL FITCH, 38, venture-backed, suit that cost more than some teachers' monthly salary, sits near the back with a tablet. He types something.
JOEL
You're describing a UX problem.
MARCUS
I'm describing a philosophy problem that expressed itself as a UX problem.
JOEL
That's a distinction without—
MARCUS
If you build a better steering wheel for a car that's pointed at a wall, the UX problem is not the steering wheel. That's the distinction.
PRIYA NAIR, 34, seventh-grade science teacher, hand goes up. She's been waiting.
PRIYA
Can I ask — when you say the technology should "get out of the way," what does that mean in a classroom where I have thirty-one kids, twenty-two devices that actually work, and a pacing guide I'm contractually obligated to follow?
MARCUS
It means—
PRIYA
Because I have heard "get out of the way" from six different startups in the last four years, and what it always seems to mean is "we're going to replace the part of your job that you're actually good at with something that learns slower than you do and costs more."
The room responds to that. Marcus holds up a hand — not defensively. Acknowledging it.
MARCUS
You're not wrong.
PRIYA
I know I'm not wrong.
MARCUS
You are the best diagnostic instrument in that room. You walk in on a Tuesday and you know — before the bell rings, before anyone opens a laptop — you know which of your thirty-one kids had a hard night. No model knows that. No platform surfaces that. You know it because you're a human being in a room with other human beings, and that is—
PRIYA
So why are we talking about AI?
MARCUS
Because you also spend four hours on Sunday generating differentiated worksheets for seven reading levels and I think that's a waste of your Sunday.
Beat. She doesn't argue with that.
DIANE
How do you train teachers to use it?
MARCUS
You don't train teachers. You're asking me how you train teachers?
DIANE
I'm asking you how you train teachers.
MARCUS
You build tools that feel like the things teachers already reach for. You make the learning curve a speed bump, not a wall. And then — this is the part that costs money and takes time and nobody wants to hear — you give teachers the chance to play with it before you ask them to perform with it.
DIANE
Define "play."
MARCUS
Unstructured time. No deliverable. No observation. No one grading them on their technology integration. You give a teacher an afternoon and you say — break it. Try something stupid. See what it can't do. Because when a teacher knows what a tool can't do, they stop being afraid of it.
JOEL
That doesn't scale.
MARCUS
Teaching doesn't scale. That's the whole — that's the — teaching doesn't scale. It compounds. You build capacity in a person and that person stands in front of thirty kids a day for thirty years and the math on that is extraordinary. But it is not a hockey stick, and if you need a hockey stick—
JOEL
I'm just saying from an adoption standpoint—
MARCUS
I know what you're saying. I've said it. I said it for six years at my first company, and we had great adoption numbers and we had teachers who could navigate our interface in their sleep and we had not one measurable improvement in what kids were learning or how they felt about learning it. We had engagement metrics. We did not have engaged students. And I went home and I thought — what did we actually do?
A long moment. The room is quieter than it was.
PRIYA
So what do you do now?
MARCUS
Now I ask better questions. Now I sit in classrooms. Now I have a team where half the people have actually taught, which sounds like a low bar and is somehow still controversial in this industry.
He walks back toward the screen. He clicks. A new slide: a single sentence.
"Technology should make teachers more themselves, not less."
MARCUS (CONT'D)
I asked you to hold something. That moment — a student surprised you and you thought, oh, there you are. That moment is the whole job. Everything we build should protect that moment. Everything we build that threatens that moment is not a solution. It is a very expensive problem.
He sets down the clicker.
MARCUS (CONT'D)
I'll take questions.
Five hands go up. Marcus points to a man in the third row — DR. SAMUEL PARK, 49, curriculum director, the kind of person who reads the footnotes.
DR. PARK
Everything you've said so far — I agree with most of it. But you've been careful not to say what you actually think AI does in the classroom. You've said what it doesn't do. What does it do?
MARCUS
It does what you tell it to do. That's the whole thing, Sam — can I call you Sam?
DR. PARK
You can call me Sam.
MARCUS
AI is extraordinarily good at doing what you tell it to do. It is not good at knowing what to tell itself to do. It has no curiosity. It has no social intelligence. It has no inner life that generates a sense of purpose. Those things are not bugs in the system that a future version will patch. They are category differences between what a machine is and what a person is.
JOEL
So you're saying AI is just a tool.
MARCUS
I'm saying a hammer is just a tool. AI is a collaborator that happens to be incompleteable without a human in the room. That's a different thing.
JOEL
That's a semantic—
MARCUS
A hammer doesn't get better or worse based on who picks it up. The same AI prompt, given by two different people with two different levels of judgment and two different capacities to interrogate the output — those are two completely different tools. The human is a variable in the system. That matters.
He clicks. The screen changes. A single data point.
MARCUS (CONT'D)
There's a principle — and it's been around since at least 2019, it's not mine, I did not invent it — the observation is that humans and computers working together consistently outperform either one working alone. That was an observation for a while. It is now a design principle. The question that follows from it — the one nobody was asking loudly enough — is: what does the partnership require from both sides?
PRIYA
It requires a functioning internet connection and a planning period longer than twenty-two minutes, which — I'm being a little facetious but not entirely.
MARCUS
You're not wrong, and we'll get there. But I want to talk about something that a neuroscientist named Vivienne Ming wrote earlier this year — Robot-Proof. Ming's argument — and she frames it as the Cyborg model, which is a word that makes some people uncomfortable and I understand that, but stay with it — her argument is that there is almost no skill AI won't eventually surpass, on its own terms. Almost none. And if you're building curriculum designed to help students beat AI at AI's game, you have already lost. You were never going to win that. The goal is not to outcompete AI.
DR. PARK
Then what's the goal?
MARCUS
To be worth keeping in the loop.
Quiet. That one lands differently.
DIANE
Say that again.
MARCUS
We are not preparing students to outcompete AI. We are preparing students to be worth keeping in the loop. That is a fundamentally different goal than what most curriculum has ever been designed to accomplish. And it requires — fundamentally — a different approach to what we teach and how we assess it.
PRIYA
Worth keeping in the loop for what? For whose benefit?
MARCUS
Both. The human's and the system's. Because here is what the research is starting to show — and this one surprised me, I want to be honest that this one surprised me — keeping a human actively in the loop doesn't just make AI safer. It makes the AI perform better.
JOEL
That's backwards.
MARCUS
Is it?
JOEL
An AI doesn't perform better because a human is watching it. That's not how—
MARCUS
Stanford HAI published a study in 2022 — Zou and colleagues, I can send you the citation — they analyzed thousands of human-AI interactions and they found something that flies in the face of every assumption I had about what makes AI useful. When the AI expressed its confidence at a mathematically accurate but moderate level, humans tended to ignore the advice. Even when the advice was good. When the AI overstated its confidence — and I want to be clear, I am not recommending we build overconfident AI — but when AI expressed higher confidence, humans engaged more and made better decisions overall.
DR. PARK
So humans need the AI to be wrong in the right direction.
MARCUS
They need the AI to communicate in a way that matches how humans actually process confidence and uncertainty. Which is not how algorithms benchmark themselves. Which means an AI optimized purely for accuracy is not necessarily the most useful AI for a human to work with. You have to design for the partnership. You can't just build a smart system and hand it to a person and say — collaborate. That's not a collaboration, that's a handoff.
PRIYA
So human-in-the-loop isn't just a guardrail.
MARCUS
No. That is my whole point and you just said it better than I did. Human-in-the-loop is not a safety net. It is not a regulatory checkbox. It is the condition under which the system works best. The human's active participation is what makes the AI more valuable — not just more acceptable, more valuable. Remove the human and you don't have a safer system. You have a dumber one.
DIANE leans forward. She is doing math in her head.
DIANE
So if the human in the loop has to be actively engaged — genuinely engaged, not rubber-stamping — then the quality of that human matters enormously.
MARCUS
Yes.
DIANE
And if we don't develop that quality in people—
MARCUS
Then we get passive humans approving AI output they don't understand, which isn't human-in-the-loop — it's human-as-rubber-stamp — and the system fails. Slowly or quickly depending on the stakes, but it fails.
PRIYA
You said something earlier about friction. Cognitive friction. What did you mean?
MARCUS
There is a temptation — it is a powerful temptation, I have felt it, every designer has felt it — to eliminate friction from the experience. Make it smooth. Make it intuitive. Make it so a student can get from question to answer without resistance. And that is exactly wrong.
PRIYA
Because—
MARCUS
Because the friction is where the learning is. The moment between "the AI said this" and "I'm not sure that's right" — that moment of interrogation, of pushing back, of synthesizing the output with your own understanding — that is not an obstacle to the collaboration. That is the collaboration. A student who knows how to look at an AI response and ask "how confident are you actually, and why" — that student is not just AI literate. That student is the ideal AI partner. That is the human worth keeping in the loop.
He clicks again. A new slide. Two lines of text:
"Demand for skills that make humans effective AI partners is rising."
"We are not building those people fast enough."
MARCUS (CONT'D)
Ojanen published a white paper — I read it on a plane last week, I almost missed my connection — looking at workforce readiness and human-AI collaboration across industries. The half-life of technical skills is shrinking. The skills that hold their value — critical thinking, adaptability, the ability to communicate and reflect and adjust — those are the exact same skills that make the Cyborg model work. And we are not developing them fast enough. The demand is accelerating. The pipeline is not.
DR. PARK
Whose responsibility is that?
MARCUS
Everybody's. Ours, specifically, in this room. But I'll tell you where the leverage is — it's here.
He gestures at the teachers in the room.
MARCUS (CONT'D)
Because a teacher who understands what it means to collaborate with AI — who knows how to model that interrogation, who can show a student not just how to use the tool but how to push back on it, how to hold the tension between what the algorithm says and what your gut says and what the evidence says — that teacher is not replaceable. That teacher is the whole plan. The technology doesn't produce the human worth keeping in the loop. You do.
He sets down the clicker again. The room is still.
MARCUS (CONT'D)
So when I said earlier that technology should make teachers more themselves — this is what I meant. Not more efficient in ways that are quantifiable. More themselves in ways that compound. More able to create the conditions where a student looks at AI output and thinks — wait. Hold on. I want to understand this before I accept it. That student is the one education has always been trying to produce. We just didn't know until recently that producing that student was going to be one of the most important things any institution on earth could do. We know now.
A long beat.
PRIYA
I'm still going to need a planning period longer than twenty-two minutes.
MARCUS
smiling
I know. I'm working on it.
The room starts to move. People stand. But DIANE stays seated, looking at the slide still on the screen. MARCUS notices.
DIANE
My best teacher — she retired two years ago, thirty-four years in the classroom — I asked her once what she thought made a good student. She said, "the ones who ask me something I haven't thought about yet."
MARCUS
Yeah.
DIANE
That's your friction.
MARCUS
That's my friction. That's the whole thing.
She nods. She picks up her bag. She leaves.
Marcus stands at the front of the room alone for a moment, looking at the slide.
He turns it off.
