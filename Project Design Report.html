Section 1. Executive Summary

Problem Overview: Routine presentation technology failures in classroom settings present a persistent, systemic threat to instructional continuity, consuming up to nine percent of planned learning time, which translates to over two hundred hours of academic downtime per school annually. Findings from current educational technology literature demonstrate that while educators possess profound pedagogical capabilities, they are rarely equipped with fundamental diagnostic skills regarding physical presentation hardware and local device configuration settings, such as mismatched resolutions, aspect ratios, or broken signal paths. Gaps in traditional administrative systems compound this issue, as schools rely almost exclusively on lagging IT ticketing support mechanisms or purely software-focused workshops that fail to teach a teacher how to physically diagnose a hardware handshake failure.

Proposed Solution and Innovation: To actively mitigate this classroom loss, this project introduces an AI-enhanced professional development curriculum paired with an interactive, real-time AI FAQ troubleshooting agent. Rooted in Bandura's self-efficacy theory and the Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK) framework, our approach empowers teachers to address physical interface errors immediately rather than succumbing to performance anxiety or technology avoidance. This dual-force intervention bridges the gap by translating complex, technical AV engineering parameters into localized, plain-language troubleshooting steps that teachers can instantly execute during a live lesson.

Expected Impact and Benefits: The proposed system establishes predictable teacher self-efficacy, converting technical helplessness into instructional agency and allowing schools to reclaim vital, compromised learning minutes. Furthermore, this localized model supports digital equity and accessibility across resource-constrained schools where dedicated IT support technicians are heavily backlogged or entirely absent. On an institutional scale, by earning a targeted SUNY CPD digital micro-credential badge, teachers form a highly resilient first line of technological defense, preserving the environmental trust necessary to utilize modern media assets.

Implementation Approach: The development process utilizes the robust ADDIE instructional design framework to systematically analyze, design, and validate the troubleshooting curriculum and accompanying AI tooling. Crucially, to test this implementation without putting live learners at risk, the project employs a decoupled, multi-platform AI sandbox loop to evaluate efficacy and prevent developer bias: Microsoft Copilot architects the training outline, Google Gemini simulates fifty randomized real-world hardware failure scenarios across five grade-level bands, and Anthropic Claude serves as an independent, blind evaluator to score time-recovery potential based on a modified Kirkpatrick framework.

Section 2. Learning Experience Design (LXD)

The Learner Journey: Educators embark on their professional development path by accessing a micro-learning portal dedicated to physical classroom AV operations. Upon entering, learners navigate modular, high-contrast visual guides focusing on three fundamental technical domains: physical cable identification, port interfaces, and device output configuration settings. During critical moments of live instruction when display glitches occur, educators transition to using the conversational AI FAQ troubleshooting agent, utilizing natural, colloquial language to describe symptoms, receiving step-by-step diagnostic feedback on their phones or tablets. Progression is structured through diagnostic scenarios of increasing complexity, culminating in simulated terminal challenges that reward teachers with a SUNY CPD digital badge upon demonstrating complete technical self-reliance.

The Pedagogical Design Elements: The learning design integrates experiential learning models and cognitive scaffolding to ensure physical competencies transfer smoothly to stressful, live classroom environments. By mapping the curriculum directly to standard ISTE educator guidelines, the design validates technical troubleshooting as an essential professional competency. To accommodate diverse user needs, the curriculum incorporates Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles, presenting troubleshooting parameters through multi-modal representations including structured text, diagrammatic signal pathways, and conversational audio feedback.

Section 3. Design Specs

AI Integration and Functionality: This system integrates generative AI across three distinct operational layers to maintain objective validation metrics and deliver robust interactive experiences. During the development phase, Microsoft Copilot defines the structural curriculum, while Google Gemini translates specialized AV engineering data into exactly 50 grade-stratified, physical-only failure scenarios across five cohorts: K-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-12, and Higher Education. In the runtime environment, the conversational AI FAQ agent uses a highly restricted retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) framework, matching user input terms (such as "projector blinking" or "purple screen") to locked, verified technical solutions while entirely suppressing hallucinations.

Ethical Considerations of Data and Privacy: Data protection protocols are strictly mapped to ensure no personally identifiable information (PII) or student metadata is ever ingested by the AI models. The system logs only anonymized technical error parameters and diagnostic pathways to identify patterns of structural failure across campus environments. To guarantee continuity during network outages, a localized, physical quick-reference card system acts as a manual fallback. If the AI agent exhausts its troubleshooting matrix without resolving the issue, it triggers an automatic escalation protocol, generating an immediate IT helpdesk ticket containing the precise diagnostic steps already performed to minimize human technician intervention latency.

Section 4. Implementation Timeline

Implementation Timeline: We will engage in rapid prototype development next week, building out our primary course structure and launching the conversational agent interface to establish the initial diagnostic platform. Following this, the project enters full implementation in week 5, executing the automated cross-platform sandbox loop by passing the 50 grade-stratified failure scenarios through our AI testing matrices to gauge baseline resolution times. Finally, we will conclude with iteration and evaluation in week 6, processing the analytical outputs through Anthropic Claude to determine the final system efficacy scores, identify any cohort-specific trends, and prepare the definitive research report for administrative review.

🎯

Section 5. Evaluation Plan

Success Criteria: System performance is quantified using a multi-layered framework designed to measure both execution reliability and pedagogical impact across classrooms. The chief target metric is an 85% or higher complete resolution rate (achieving a score of 3 on our time-recovery rubric) across all 50 simulated scenarios. Additionally, we track key performance indicators including average time-to-resolution (striving for under 120 seconds), a reduction in repeat IT helpdesk tickets for identical physical failures, and an increase in teacher comfort levels. A critical limitation of this simulated testing framework is that while it accurately validates technical logic, it cannot account for real-world variables such as physical motor skill deficits or human-induced physical damage.

Metrics and Data Collection: Due to systemic time constraints, we will plan for evaluation and establish the metrics infrastructure, but will not collect live learner data. The analytical models will log quantitative performance variables such as baseline versus post-remediation resolution times, categorical resolution rates across the 5 cohorts, and escalation frequencies for unresolvable errors. For qualitative insights, we have designed feedback loops including diagnostic transcripts and immediate, post-resolution user satisfaction surveys. These combined metrics will be compiled into clear comparative charts, providing institutional decision-makers with a highly visible, empirical argument for widespread training adoption.
